Founding Partner

Brian Arnott

Brian Arnott is the founding partner of Novita Interpares and he has led all the company’s cultural projects.

In the field of heritage consulting, Brian is a Founder and past National President of the Canadian Association of Heritage Professionals. Brian led Novita’s team on two living heritage museums, the Ukrainian Cultural Heritage Village in Alberta and The Petrolia Discovery in Ontario and he is a specialist in several areas of Canadian industrial history. He recently made a presentation on the founding of Lunenburg at the annual conference of the National Trust for Canada.

Brian has produced documentaries for the CBC and NFB and has created exhibitions for the ROM and National Gallery of Canada and he has written widely on cultural policy.

In addition to his leadership of Novita Interpares, Brian Arnott has worked in professional theatre in Canada and the US as a production manager and company manager. He worked for the National Theatre and the Royal Shakespeare Company in London has designed costumes, scenery and lighting for more than 40 stage productions in Canada. He was the drama critic for That’s Showbusiness for four years and Maclean’s Magazine for one year.

Brian Arnott is widely recognized as a leading expert in North America on the design of theatres and facilities for live performance. In this capacity, he has been a visiting lecturer and guest critic at many schools of architecture. As Senior Partner and Head of Design for Novita’s Technical Division from 1972 until 2010, Brian has been responsible for the planning and design of more than 100 live performance venues in Canada, the US and abroad.

As a volunteer in the cultural sector, Brian has been a member of the Board of Directors of Comus Music Theatre, The Pleiades Theatre, Factory Theatre (as a Director and as Chair of the Board, Chair of the Advisory Committee for the Theatre Program at Humber College and Chair of the Advisory Committee for the Heritage Carpentry Program at NSCC Lunenburg. Brian was also a member of the federal government task force on the Heritage Industries Labour Force in Canada.

Brian has written widely on cultural and heritage policy and practice and is a regular contributor to Municipal World. Brian recently published a book entitled “Learning Lunenburg: 100 Ways of Being in a Small Community.” Brian was educated at Bishop’s University, the University of British Columbia and the University of Alberta.
